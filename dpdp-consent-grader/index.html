How it grades — the method, in the open

Rule-based, not a black box

Each check looks for the presence — or absence — of a specific element the Act requires a notice to contain. The logic is transparent and inspectable; there is no opaque score.

Tied to the statute

Every verdict cites the section it answers to: §5 (notice), §6 (the standard of consent), §7 (legitimate uses), §8 (Data Fiduciary duties). A flag you can trace is a flag you can defend.

A screener, not an opinion

It catches the structural failures that appear again and again — missing withdrawal routes, bundled consent, no grievance officer. It does not replace a lawyer's reading of the specific facts.

On its limits: the grader reads text for required elements; it cannot judge whether a stated purpose is genuinely necessary, whether consent was freely given in practice, or how the notice operates in the actual product flow. Those are legal judgments. Treat a high grade as “clears the obvious bar,” not “compliant” — and a low grade as “has structural gaps worth a closer look.”
